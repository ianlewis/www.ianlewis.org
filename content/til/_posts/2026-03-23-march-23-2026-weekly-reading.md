## Software Complexity

- [Most of What We Call Progress](https://yusufaytas.com/most-of-what-we-call-progress/?utm_source=tldrwebdev) -- _Yusuf Aytaş_

    Yusuf argues that adding complexity to software is often mistaken for
    progress. This is a fairly common misconception that's been written about a
    lot. The article made me realize, though, that "complexity" isn't really the
    problem, it's "manageability".

    I've personally more often encountered software that didn't accomplish what
    I wanted or was outright broken than software that I felt was simply too
    complex. The key is having just the right amount of complexity to solve the
    problem at hand, while also maintaining enough flexibility to adapt to
    likely future change.

    The real problem is whether the solution is manageable; does it solve the
    problem? Is it flexible enough to adapt to likely future change? Is it easy
    to maintain relative to the benefits it provides? I don't think these ideas
    are really captured by the term "complexity".

- [Nobody Gets Promoted for Simplicity](https://terriblesoftware.org/2026/03/03/nobody-gets-promoted-for-simplicity/) -- _Matheus Lima_

    Matheus also makes the case for simplicity. He makes the point that folks
    are often rewarded for more complex solutions. This might be true, but my
    own experience is that promotion committees aren't looking at the complexity
    alone. They are looking at the value provided by the solution.

    I do agree though, that if the impact is very high but the effort required
    was very low then it won't be evaluated as highly. This might be the true
    issue. This mostly becomes a documentation problem. Engineers in this
    situation should put a lot of emphasis on the upfront work done in making
    the decision to go with a simpler solution.

## Staying Healthy as an Introvert

- [Social Ties Help You Live Longer. What Does That Mean for
  Introverts?](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/09/well/introvert-longevity-tips.html)
  -- _Dana G. Smith_

    This is a good article about how introverts can maintain their health as
    they get older. Being introverted can create some headwinds as you get older
    because social ties have health benefits and it's easier to maintain them if
    you're extroverted.

    One thing that the article points out that is often overlooked is how social
    connections can help you maintain motivation to maintain healthy habits. I
    find that if I only have to worry about my self-image, then I am more likely
    to let myself go. If I have a partner or friends, that makes me care a bit
    more about how I look and feel.
