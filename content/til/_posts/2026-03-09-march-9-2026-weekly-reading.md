## Artificial Intelligence & Security

- [A GitHub Issue Title Compromised 4,000 Developer Machines](https://grith.ai/blog/clinejection-when-your-ai-tool-installs-another) -- _grith team_

    The [Cline project](https://github.com/cline/cline) on GitHub was
    compromised through prompt injection and cache poisoning. The attack started
    with an issue containing instructions hidden in an issue title which
    instructed the AI triage bot to run malicious code to poison the CI cache.

    A number of mistakes were made:
    - Cline used the cache for release workflows. CI caches can be poisoned
      which could lead to compromise of release artifacts. In the
      [post-mortem](https://cline.bot/blog/post-mortem-unauthorized-cline-cli-npm)
      they mention removing GitHub Actions cache from workflows that handle
      publication credentials. I hope that includes GitHub releases.
    - A security researcher who found the cache poisoning vulnerability
      responsibly disclosed it to the Cline team but they didn't respond or fix
      the issue for almost two months.
    - The security researcher published a proof of concept exploit to a test
      repository. The attacker found it and used it to do the attack. This
      shouldn't have been published publicly without a fix in place.
    - The Cline team removed the wrong token, leaving a compromised token in
      place. Even after they discovered it and re-rotated it, the compromised
      token remained valid. I suppose they didn't revoke it.

## Artificial Intelligence & Software Development

- [Change Intent Records: The Missing Artifact in AI-Assisted Development](https://blog.bryanl.dev/posts/change-intent-records/) -- _Bryan Liles_

    Bryan introduces the idea of Change Intent Records (CIRs) which are a way to capture
    the intent behind a change in code, mostly for AI consumption. CIRs are
    similar to Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) but are focused on capturing
    the intent behind the implementation of a particular change.

    It struck me as a very strange proposal. First, why is this important now? I
    think more structured documentation for changes would have been great even
    before AI-assisted development.

    Second, we already have commit messages and issues. Bryan argues that
    commit messages should capture _what_ changed, but CIRs should capture
    _why_ it changed. I think this is wrong. Git messages should absolutely
    capture why something has changed. It's only our current bad habits of
    making very granular uncurated commit messages that make something like
    this necessary. PRs should always have been one-commit. GitHub was always
    just too lazy to manage changes between patches outside of git commits
    themselves.

    I really feel like issue and PR comments should track the history behind a
    change. Git commits should be better structured and capture the final
    summary of what changed and why.

- [<!-- textlint-disable spelling -->Evaluating AGENTS.md: Are Repository-Level Context Files Helpful for Coding Agents?<!-- textlint-enable spelling -->](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.11988) -- _Thibaud Gloaguen, Niels Mündler, Mark Müller, Veselin Raychev, Martin Vechev_

    This is an interesting paper that evaluates the usefulness of
    repository-level context files e.g. `AGENTS.md` for coding agents.

    The finding that they are most often not helpful is perhaps unintuitive but
    makes sense if you think about it. Most `AGENTS.md` files are generated by
    the LLM themselves. Thus, they don't contain any information that the LLM
    doesn't already have in the training data or in the repository itself.

## Artificial Intelligence & Ethics

- [Anthropic ditches its core safety promise in the middle of an AI red line fight with the Pentagon](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/25/tech/anthropic-safety-policy-change) -- _Clare Duffy, CNN_

    Many folks started using Claude, and many upgraded their plans in response
    to Anthropic's principled stance that
    [I wrote about last week](/til/2026/03/02/march-2-2026-weekly-reading).

    [![Liz Fong-Jones writes oh, I was on Pro 1x before, but I switched to 20x to show support for Anthropic after Hegseth tried to fuck them over](/assets/images/2026-03-09-march-9-2026-weekly-reading/lizthegrey_com_3mfx4jdyng223.png){: .align-center}](https://bsky.app/profile/lizthegrey.com/post/3mfx4jdyng223)

    I thought at the time that it was a bit too early to start praising
    Anthropic for their moral stances, and it seems I was mostly right.

    Anthropic saying they are "Separating our plans as a company from our
    recommendations for the industry" is exactly why folks don't trust
    companies. They always defer to the law and capitalism because if they
    aren't perceived as [maximizing shareholder
    value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shareholder_value) then they risk being
    replaced by investors. If they have any kind of morals, they won't let that
    affect their business practices.

    Saying of their safety roadmap, that "Rather than being hard
    commitments, these are public goals that we will openly grade our progress
    towards." My feeling is that this is the new
    ["Don't be Evil"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don%27t_be_evil) promise
    that will never really be followed and will eventually be quietly removed
    from the website.
